<p>Dear QGIS users,</p>
<p>As you know, QGIS 3 has recently been published. This version introduced big changes in  the code structure that, in addition to the new functionalities already exposed, makes our code base more modern and easier to expand and improve on in the future.</p>
<p>As a normal by-product of such a huge overhaul, these changes also triggered a series of new issues, that you, our users are helping to discover and document. Our objective is to eliminate the most important of these issues in time for what will be our next Long Term Release (LTR) &#8211; version 3.4. This release is <a href="https://qgis.org/en/site/getinvolved/development/roadmap.html#release-schedule">scheduled for October 2018</a>. The resources available from <a href="http://QGIS.ORG">QGIS.ORG</a> funds are limited, and we have already invested in QGIS 3.0 far more than we have done for any previous version.</p>
<p>Now is a great time for users, and particularly for power users, larger institutions and enterprises, to invest in QGIS bugfixing. You have a number of different options: donating your developers&#8217; time or hiring a developer directly to resolve the bugs that annoy you most, <a href="https://qgis.org/en/site/getinvolved/governance/sponsorship/sponsorship.html#qgis-sponsorship-program">sponsoring our foundation</a>, or <a href="https://qgis.org/en/site/getinvolved/donations.html">donating</a> to <a href="http://QGIS.ORG">QGIS.ORG</a>.</p>
<p>Our targets are:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="">20k€ within 2018-05-18 (for 3.2)</span></li>
<li><span class="">40k€ within 2018-09-14 (for 3.4)</span></li>
</ul>
<p>If you would like to help, feel free to contact us (preferably through the qgis-users or qgis-developers mailing list, or directly to info@qgis.org) for further details!</p>
