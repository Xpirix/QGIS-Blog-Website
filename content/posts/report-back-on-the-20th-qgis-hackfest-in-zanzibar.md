<p>This post summarizes personal reports of community members who attended the 20<sup>th</sup> Hackfest in Stone Town, Zanzibar the week before the FOSS4G in Dar Es Salaam.</p>
<h2><strong>Report from Matteo Ghetta</strong></h2>
<p>QGIS developers spent 3 days in the beautiful island of Zanzibar were they worked on bug fixing, improvement of new features and documentation enhancements. It is extremely important for developers to meet together and spend some days working side by side given that the geographical distribution is very wide.</p>
<p>Thanks to the (local government &amp; Yves Barthelemy) we had the chance to visit the Land Mapping Commission in Stone Town: It has been an amazing experience for both developers and local council. Moreover developers visited the SUZA (University of Zanzibar) and saw the local infrastructure and GIS related workflow.</p>
<ul>
<li>I have merged many PR of the documentation</li>
<li>Many old and not old issues have been closed or the author has been pinged</li>
<li>Many new additions to the documentation both small corrections and new sections from scratch</li>
<li>A new version of DataPlotly released</li>
<li>A small presentation of DataPlotly to the QGIS crew</li>
</ul>
<h2>Report from Mario Baranzini</h2>
<p>I spent one day of the Hackfest contributing to the SLYR project, improving the conversion of LAB colors. SLYR (<a href="https://github.com/nyalldawson/slyr">https://github.com/nyalldawson/slyr</a>) is a project started and almost entirely developed by Nyall (which was not present to the Hackfest) which aims to provide tools to extract and convert symbols from ESRI .lyr and .style files and use them in QGIS. Nyall has recently worked hard on the project and perhaps soon sponsorship will allow further development.</p>
<p>Around this theme there is a keen interest. Even during the FOSS4G there has been discussion of the conversion of ESRI symbols and there was also a presentation of how the conversion in Israel is currently managed.</p>
<p>During the rest of the hackfest, I worked on the implementation of the new QField file selector.</p>
<h2>Report from Denis Rouzaud</h2>
<ul>
<li>I refreshed the <a href="https://qgis.org/pyqgis/master/">Python API documentation</a> with latest versions (3.2 and master).</li>
<li>I implemented the <a href="https://github.com/qgis/QGIS/pull/7734">notification system on Mac OS</a></li>
<li>I planned a Mac OS dedicated bug-fixing programme (<a href="http://blog.qgis.org/2018/09/12/macosx-specific-bug-fixing-campaign/">now successfully launched</a>) and discussed the future of the release process for Mac.</li>
<li>I implemented <a href="https://github.com/opengisch/QField/pull/348">geometry editing in QField</a> (aka QGIS mobile)</li>
<li>I improved the vector layer properties dialog by creating a single <a href="https://github.com/qgis/QGIS/pull/7752">table for layer capabilities</a> (editable, identifiable, removable, read-only).</li>
</ul>
<h2>Report from Tim Sutton</h2>
<p>Firstly let me thank the QGIS project for contributing to my travel and accommodation costs &#8211; I am most grateful for the support! This hackfest was special for me because it is the first hackfest in an African country. As an African born person living in the interface between &#8220;first world&#8221; and &#8220;third world&#8221;, I have always had a particular social agenda with QGIS: To bring spatial tools to support responsible and sustainable management of our world. There is a huge technological and skills divide between Europe and less developed societies where QGIS can be a valuable social enabler in helping to advance the standard of living and quality of life. Hosting a hackfest in an environment where we don&#8217;t have tree cadastres, street furniture cadastres and every aspect of civilian life mapped and systematised is an important way to build empathy and understanding in our QGIS core community members for a broader cross-section of our user base. Having the opportunity to meet with users from ZMI (the Zanzibar Mapping Initiative) and students from the local university was a really uplifting experience. ZMI are building the national cadastre from the ground up using QGIS, PostGIS and UAV mapping. We were privileged to have a number of Zanzibar residents join us on the hackfest and get to experience just how appreciative they are, first hand.</p>
<p>There were a number of interesting topics that arose during the hackfest which I will try to summarise here:</p>
<p><strong>Certification:</strong></p>
<p>We held an extended meeting on the QGIS Certification Programme, mainly detailing how we should go about the review process for onboarding new organisations.</p>
<p><strong>Mac OS Build:</strong></p>
<p>I spent quite a bit of time struggling with my MacOS build on QGIS. It&#8217;s definitely an area of the project that needs more work as the process can be non-trivial and the brew based formulas quickly become outdated.</p>
<p><strong>QField Show and Tell:</strong></p>
<p>Matthias showed off the latest version of QField and all the hard work they have been putting into the QGIS mobile client. This Android based version of QGIS for mobile data gathering is a really great project and is getting more and more useful with each release. There was also a translation sprint for QField during which I translated it to Afrikaans.</p>
<p><strong>QField Autobuilds:</strong></p>
<p>Denis and Matthias showed off the work they have done to automate the .apk builds for QField. Their system combines Travis and some git hooks to automatically build .APK&#8217;s whenever a pull request os made or a tag is made. See the QField travis for details: <a href="https://github.com/opengisch/QField/blob/master/.travis.yml">https://github.com/opengisch/QField/blob/master/.travis.yml</a></p>
<p><strong>Plotly plugin:</strong></p>
<p>Matteo Ghetta showed off the latest capabilities of the Data Plotly plugin for QGIS 3. The plugin supports the creation of a wide assortment of charts from your layer&#8217;s attribute data. See the plugin homepage here for examples and more detail: <a href="https://github.com/ghtmtt/DataPlotly">https://github.com/ghtmtt/DataPlotly</a><span class="Apple-converted-space"> </span></p>
<p><strong>Governance:</strong></p>
<p>I took the opportunity to sign off the statute changes from 2017 general meeting. This was my last official act as outgoing project Chair. I was also extremely humbled to be awarded an Honorary PSC membership during the FOSS4G2018 conference.</p>

<strong>Easter Eggs:</strong></p>
<p>I contributed a couple of new easter eggs to QGIS. While easter eggs are fun, the data behind these hidden tools provide an important history of the project. I took the opportunity of having many long-time QGIS community members around the dinner table to collate all the previous QGIS meet-up dates. You can view this as a map here: <a href="https://github.com/qgis/QGIS/blob/master/resources/data/qgis-hackfests.json">https://github.com/qgis/QGIS/blob/master/resources/data/qgis-hackfests.json</a><span class="Apple-converted-space"> </span></p>
<p>The spatial clustering of these events shows that going to Zanzibar and getting out of our geographical comfort zone is a really useful endeavour.</p>
<h2>Report from Paolo Cavallini</h2>

<h2>Report from Matthias Kuhn</h2>
<p>The highest value of hackfests is actually to meet and greet and discuss ideas and bigger plans. There is a lot of communication on a high bandwidth channel (also known as face-to-face) that strengthens the community. This helps to quickly overcome technical problems sometimes. Or to give someone some tips and tricks you normally wouldn&#8217;t come to (e.g. optimizing a git workflow) that results in a long-term gain because of sustainable productivity improvements. I tend to walk around once in a while and just randomly bump into people where I &#8211; more or less successfully &#8211; try to help them solve their issues or discuss approaches. In the same area, there is the possibility to informally discuss plans about organization, workflows and strategies. The concept of an LTR was to a big degree discussed and built at a hackfest in Essen some years ago. This year there was a very interesting discussion about the topic LTR. For how much time the &#8220;long&#8221; in the term &#8220;long term release&#8221; should stand, about the life cycle of releases and of long-term releases. One of the important things when it comes to this question is what organisations think and do &#8211; because, in the end, it is mostly for organisations that the LTR exists &#8211; and how development resources can be assigned to the task of keeping a high quality of an LTR during its whole lifetime. There are no conclusions yet, but synchronizing on these ideas is often the seed for tomorrow&#8217;s exciting changes.</p>
<p>We were also visited by a group of students from the local university. It was really refreshing to talk to them about open source and QGIS and how we work and what the challenges of spatial data and GIS infrastructure are on an island like Zanzibar.</p>
<p>Feature-wise, the main thing I worked on feature wise was a new snap to grid functionality that is available for digitizing tools. It allows configuring a precision for vector layers. Whenever a new node is added or an existing one is edited on this vector layer, it will be automatically placed on this grid. Normally this is used to force the objects on a layer to something like cm or mm resolution.</p>
<p>And then, of course, I did my daily bunch of (mostly volunteer) pull request reviewing, some code cleanup and some bugfixes.</p>
